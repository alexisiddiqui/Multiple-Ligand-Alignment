
# BACKGROUND.md

## Per-Atom Confidence for Ligands: From MSA Depth to Multiple Ligand Alignment

---

### The Problem

Structure prediction models produce per-residue confidence scores for
proteins (pLDDT in AlphaFold, pTM in ESMFold). These scores are deeply
useful — they tell you which parts of a prediction to trust and which to
treat with caution. They correlate strongly with local prediction accuracy
and have become standard filters in downstream applications like virtual
screening, docking, and molecular dynamics.

For ligands, no equivalent exists. When a model predicts a ligand binding
pose or generates a novel molecule, every atom is treated with equal
confidence. There is no per-atom signal that says "this carbon's placement
is well-supported by known chemistry" versus "this nitrogen is in
uncharted territory."

This gap matters. Drug-like molecules are not uniform objects. A molecule
might have a well-characterised aromatic scaffold (seen in thousands of
known bioactive compounds) connected to a novel R-group (seen in almost
none). A per-atom confidence score that captures this distinction would
be immediately useful for:

- **Docking**: Weighting pose scoring by atom reliability
- **Generative models**: Identifying which parts of a generated molecule
  are well-grounded versus speculative
- **Lead optimisation**: Knowing where you have SAR coverage and where
  you're flying blind
- **Binding affinity prediction**: Downweighting contributions from
  poorly-characterised atoms

---

### The MSA Analogy

The insight behind this work is that protein confidence scores are not
magic — they are fundamentally measures of **evolutionary support**. A
residue with high pLDDT is one where the multiple sequence alignment (MSA)
provides abundant, diverse evidence for what that position should look like.

The key quantity is **Neff** (effective number of sequences), computed
per-residue from the MSA. At its core, Neff answers:

> "How many independent observations support the identity and local
> environment of this residue?"

Neff is not simply the raw count of sequences in the MSA. Sequences are
weighted to account for redundancy — a cluster of 500 nearly identical
homologs contributes less than 10 diverse sequences covering the same
position. This weighting, typically Inverse Degree-style, ensures that Neff
reflects genuine evolutionary diversity rather than sampling bias.

The correlation between MSA depth and prediction quality is one of the
strongest empirical findings in structural biology. Regions with deep,
diverse MSAs are predicted accurately. Regions with shallow MSAs are
predicted poorly. This is not a coincidence — it reflects a fundamental
principle: **prediction confidence should be proportional to the
information content of the input data at that position**.

We hypothesise that the same principle applies to ligands.

---

### The Ligand Analog

If MSA depth drives protein confidence, what drives ligand confidence?
We need a ligand equivalent of the MSA: a collection of related molecules
that provides evidence for the chemical environment at each atom.

We call this a **Multiple Ligand Alignment (MLA)**. The analogy maps as
follows:

| Protein / MSA | Ligand / MLA |
|---------------|--------------|
| Protein sequence | Query ligand |
| Residue at position *i* | Atom at index *i* |
| Amino acid identity (type + context) | Atom-centred substructure (Morgan environment) |
| Homologous sequences in MSA | Reference ligands passing similarity filter |
| Sequence identity threshold | Tanimoto similarity threshold |
| Inverse Degree sequence weighting | Inverse Degree-style ligand weighting |
| Per-residue Neff | Per-atom Neff |

The conceptual leap is representing each atom not by its element type
alone, but by its **circular chemical environment** — the substructure
centred on that atom extending outward to a defined radius. This is
precisely what Morgan/ECFP fingerprints encode. A carbon atom in a
phenyl ring has a completely different fingerprint signature from a
carbon in a methyl group, just as an alanine in an alpha helix has a
different evolutionary profile from an alanine in a disordered loop.

### How It Works

#### Step 1: Atom-Level Fingerprint Decomposition

A Morgan fingerprint is typically computed for an entire molecule and
represented as a fixed-length bit vector. But internally, every bit in
that vector can be attributed to the specific atom (or atoms) that
generated it.

For a given atom *i* at Morgan radius *r*, the fingerprint algorithm:

1. Collects the identities of all atoms and bonds within *r* bonds of
   atom *i*
2. Hashes this substructure into one or more bit positions

The set of bits generated by atom *i* at radius *r* is its **fingerprint
signature** — a compact representation of its local chemical environment.

At radius 1, this captures immediate connectivity: element type, bond
types, and direct neighbours. At radius 2, it extends to second-shell
neighbours, capturing local topology. At radius 3, it encodes extended
neighbourhood structure, approaching the scale of pharmacophoric features.

RDKit's `AdditionalOutput.GetAtomToBits()` provides this decomposition
directly: for each atom, the set of fingerprint bits it contributes to.

#### Step 2: Reference Set Construction

Given a query ligand, we identify a set of reference ligands from a
database (either a general chemical library like ChEMBL or a
target-specific set of known actives). References are selected by global
Tanimoto similarity above a liberal threshold (typically 0.3), ensuring
broad coverage while excluding completely unrelated molecules.

This is analogous to the PSI-BLAST search that builds an MSA — casting a
wide net to gather sequences with detectable homology.

#### Step 3: Redundancy Weighting

Not all references are equally informative. A congeneric series of 200
analogs from a single medicinal chemistry campaign provides less
independent information than 20 structurally diverse compounds spanning
different scaffolds.

We apply Inverse Degree-style weighting: each reference ligand receives a
weight inversely proportional to the number of other references it is
similar to (above a clustering threshold, typically Tanimoto ≥ 0.7).
Isolated, structurally unique references get weight 1.0. Members of
large clusters of near-identical compounds get weight approaching 0.

This directly mirrors the Inverse Degree weighting used in MSA Neff, where
sequences in large families of close homologs are downweighted relative
to rare, divergent sequences.

#### Step 4: Per-Atom Neff

For each atom *i* in the query ligand, we ask:

> "How many independent reference ligands reproduce the chemical
> environment of this atom?"

Concretely, we compute the overlap between atom *i*'s fingerprint bits
and each reference ligand's full fingerprint. If a reference has most
of the bits that atom *i* generates, it "supports" that atom — meaning
the reference contains a similar local chemical environment.

The per-atom Neff is the weighted sum of these overlaps across all
references:

```
Neff(atom_i) = Σ_j  w_j × overlap(atom_i, ref_j) × 𝟙[overlap ≥ δ]
```

where `w_j` is the Inverse Degree weight and `δ` is a minimum overlap threshold
that prevents distant ligands sharing a single common bit from
contributing noise.

An atom embedded in a common pharmacophore (e.g., a hydroxyl group
hydrogen-bonding in a well-studied binding motif) will have high Neff
because many diverse reference ligands contain that environment. An atom
in a novel heterocyclic system will have low Neff because few references
reproduce its local chemistry.

#### Step 5: Multi-Radius Aggregation

We compute Neff at multiple Morgan radii (typically 1, 2, and 3) and
combine them. This captures confidence at different scales of chemical
context:

- **Radius 1**: Is the immediate connectivity well-represented?
  (Common element, typical bonding pattern)
- **Radius 2**: Is the local topology well-represented?
  (Ring membership, branching pattern, nearby functional groups)
- **Radius 3**: Is the extended neighbourhood well-represented?
  (Scaffold context, pharmacophoric arrangement)

An atom might have high Neff at radius 1 (it's a common nitrogen) but
low Neff at radius 3 (the broader structural context is unusual). The
combined score reflects overall support.

We use a shifted geometric mean for aggregation, which naturally
penalises atoms that lack support at any resolution without being
as harsh as a strict minimum.

#### Step 6: Normalisation to Confidence

Raw Neff values are database-dependent — a query against 2 million
ChEMBL compounds will yield larger raw scores than a query against 50
known actives. To produce an interpretable [0, 1] confidence score, we
apply a saturating exponential:

```
confidence(atom_i) = 1 - exp(-Neff(atom_i) / λ)
```

The saturation parameter λ is estimated adaptively from the distribution
of Neff scores for the current query, ensuring meaningful spread
regardless of database size. Atoms at the median Neff receive confidence
≈ 0.63; atoms at 3× median receive ≈ 0.95.

### Relationship to Existing Work

#### Similarity Maps

RDKit provides `SimilarityMaps.GetSimilarityMapForFingerprint()`, which
visualises per-atom contributions to the Tanimoto similarity between
**two** molecules. This is a pairwise comparison tool, useful for SAR
analysis. Our approach extends this concept from a single reference to
an entire reference population, with explicit redundancy correction.

#### Applicability Domain Methods

Applicability domain (AD) estimation in QSAR asks whether a query
molecule falls within the chemical space covered by the training set.
Common approaches include distance-to-centroid, leverage values, or
binary "in/out" classification based on global similarity thresholds.

Ligand Neff differs in three ways:

1. **Per-atom resolution**: AD methods typically give a single
   molecule-level score. We provide atom-level granularity.
2. **Weighted coverage**: AD methods often use hard cutoffs. We
   use continuous weighted overlap with redundancy correction.
3. **Multi-scale**: AD methods typically operate at one fingerprint
   resolution. We aggregate across Morgan radii to capture coverage
   at different structural scales.

#### AlphaFold and Ligand Confidence

AlphaFold3 and related models predict ligand poses with associated
confidence metrics. These are learned scores derived from the model's
internal representations. They capture something related to but distinct
from data coverage — a model might be confident about a ligand pose
because the protein-ligand interaction pattern is well-represented in
its training data, even if the specific ligand is novel.

Ligand Neff is complementary: it measures data coverage independently
of any model. It can serve as:

- An input feature to structure prediction models
- A diagnostic for interpreting model confidence
- A filter for identifying where model predictions should be treated
  with extra caution

### Design Decisions and Tradeoffs

#### Why Morgan Fingerprints?

Morgan/ECFP fingerprints are the natural choice because they are:

1. **Atom-centred**: Each bit is attributable to a specific atom (or set
   of atoms), enabling per-atom decomposition without modification.
2. **Multi-scale**: The radius parameter directly controls the size of
   the chemical environment captured. This maps cleanly to our
   multi-radius aggregation.
3. **Widely adopted**: Tanimoto similarity on Morgan fingerprints is the
   de facto standard for molecular similarity in cheminformatics.
   This means existing intuitions about similarity thresholds apply.
4. **Fast**: RDKit generates Morgan fingerprints in ~10μs per molecule.
   The bottleneck is numerical computation, not fingerprint generation.

Alternatives like MACCS keys (not atom-centred), topological torsions
(not circular), or learned fingerprints (not interpretable at the atom
level) do not support the decomposition we require.

#### Why Not Learned Representations?

Graph neural network embeddings (e.g., from GNNs or transformers) produce
per-atom representations that could, in principle, be used for a similar
coverage analysis. We chose Morgan fingerprints instead because:

1. **Interpretability**: Each Morgan bit corresponds to a concrete
   substructure. The user can inspect exactly which chemical environment
   an atom lacks coverage for.
2. **Reproducibility**: Morgan fingerprints are deterministic and
   parameter-free (given radius and bit length). Learned representations
   depend on training data and random seeds.
3. **No training required**: The package works out of the box with any
   reference database. No model training, no GPU for inference.
4. **Established similarity semantics**: Tanimoto on Morgan fingerprints
   has decades of empirical validation. Cosine similarity on GNN
   embeddings does not have the same calibrated interpretation.

That said, extending this framework to learned representations is
straightforward — the core Neff computation only requires a per-atom
feature vector and a similarity metric.

#### Coverage Metric: Overlap vs. Jaccard

We use an asymmetric overlap metric rather than Jaccard/Tanimoto for the
per-atom coverage calculation:

```
overlap(atom_i, ref_j) = |bits_i ∩ bits_j| / |bits_i|
```

This measures "what fraction of atom *i*'s environment is present in
reference *j*?" — not the symmetric "how similar are the two fingerprints?"

This is deliberate. We care about **support for this atom**, not overall
molecular similarity. A large reference molecule might share all of atom
*i*'s bits while having many additional bits that atom *i* doesn't
generate. Under Jaccard, this would yield a low score. Under overlap,
it correctly recognises full coverage of atom *i*'s environment.

This mirrors how MSA Neff treats residue identity: a sequence counts as
supporting a position if it has the same (or similar) amino acid there,
regardless of what the rest of the sequence looks like.

#### Bit Collisions

When Morgan fingerprints are folded to a fixed bit length (2048, 4096,
or 8192), different substructures may hash to the same bit position.
This means an atom may appear to be "covered" by a reference that
actually has a different substructure occupying the same bit.

This is a known limitation of any hashed fingerprint. We mitigate it by:

1. Offering larger bit vectors (4096 or 8192) to reduce collision
   probability, with 4096 recommended as the default for per-atom work
2. Using multi-radius aggregation, which requires consistent coverage
   across radii — a collision at one radius is unlikely to repeat at
   another
3. Applying a minimum overlap threshold (δ), so a single colliding bit
   from an otherwise unrelated reference is insufficient to register
   as coverage

#### Declarative Configuration via YAML

Computational chemistry pipelines are often plagued by hardcoded parameters hidden deep in scripts, making it difficult to reproduce results or systematically sweep parameters like Morgan radius or overlap thresholds.

`ligand-neff` addresses this by exposing all parameters through a single, flat declarative configuration object (`NeffConfig`) that maps directly to a user-facing YAML file. This guarantees that every Neff computation is associated with a reproducible configuration artifact. The YAML structure logically groups parameters (fingerprint definition, alignment thresholds, redundancy weighting) without complicating the internal flat dataclass used by the computation engine.

### Expected Behaviour

We anticipate that ligand Neff will exhibit the following properties,
analogous to MSA Neff:

1. **Scaffold atoms have high Neff**: Atoms in common pharmacophoric
   motifs (phenyl rings, amide bonds, common heterocycles) will be
   well-covered because they appear in many reference ligands.

2. **R-group atoms have variable Neff**: Substituent positions will
   show Neff proportional to the diversity of analogues explored at
   that position.

3. **Novel chemistry has low Neff**: Atoms in unusual ring systems,
   rare element types, or unconventional bonding patterns will have
   low Neff regardless of database size.

4. **Target-specific Neff reflects SAR coverage**: When using a
   target-specific reference set, Neff will reflect which parts of the
   molecule have been explored in medicinal chemistry campaigns versus
   which remain untested.

5. **Neff correlates with prediction accuracy**: We hypothesise that
   atoms with higher Neff will show lower RMSD in docking and higher
   accuracy in binding affinity prediction. This is the key hypothesis
   to validate.

---

### Validation Plan

The central claim — that per-atom ligand Neff correlates with prediction
confidence and accuracy — must be validated experimentally.

**Primary validation**: For a diverse set of protein-ligand complexes
with known crystal structures, dock ligands using standard methods,
compute per-atom Neff against ChEMBL, and measure the correlation
between atom-level Neff and atom-level coordinate RMSD relative to
the crystal pose. The prediction is that low-Neff atoms will have
systematically higher positional error.

**Secondary validation**: For systems where AlphaFold3 or similar
models provide per-atom ligand confidence scores, measure rank
correlation between model confidence and Neff. Agreement would suggest
that models have implicitly learned something about data coverage.
Disagreement would be equally interesting — atoms where the model is
confident but Neff is low (or vice versa) may reveal cases where
models extrapolate beyond their training distribution.

**Ablation**: Compute virtual screening performance (enrichment, AUC)
using only high-Neff atoms for the Tanimoto calculation. If Neff
correctly identifies reliable atoms, this "confident atom" screening
should match or exceed full-molecule screening.

---

### Summary

`ligand-neff` adapts a proven concept from protein structure prediction
— measuring evolutionary support per residue — to the chemical domain.
By decomposing Morgan fingerprints to the atom level and computing
weighted coverage against a reference database, we produce per-atom
confidence scores that reflect how well each atom's chemical environment
is characterised in known chemical space.

The approach is model-agnostic, requires no training, and produces
interpretable scores grounded in concrete substructural analysis. It
is designed to complement, not replace, learned confidence scores from
structure prediction models.
